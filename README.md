# TamilIdentity∞

**What does it mean to be Tamil?** — an interactive, accessible, bilingual journey through more than 3,800 years of Tamil identity.

> யாதும் ஊரே யாவரும் கேளிர் · *Yāthum ūrē yāvarum kēḷir* — “Every town is our home town; every human, our kin.”

A behavioural-change and philosophical tool for Tamils across the world — Tamil Nadu, Eelam, Malaysia, Singapore, Mauritius, South Africa, and the wider diaspora — to understand the history of their people and **build from there**.

## The four pillars

Drawn from a synthesis of Sangam poetics, the Tirukkuṟaḷ, and the Siddhar tradition, the experience is built around four pillars of what it has meant to be Tamil:

1. **Ecological consciousness** — the five landscapes (*Aintiṇai*) of Sangam poetry, where emotion is woven into land.
2. **Universal kinship & Aram** — Kaniyan Pūngundranār's borderless ethic and the secular morality of the Tirukkuṟaḷ.
3. **Radical social democracy** — the Siddhars' revolt against caste, ritual gatekeeping and hierarchy.
4. **The body as temple** — Thirumūlar's somatic mysticism: *Uḷḷam perum kōyil* — the heart is the great temple.

## Features

- **Fully bilingual** — every Tamil verse carries romanised transliteration and plain-English meaning, so readers and non-readers of Tamil meet these ideas together.
- **Interactive Aintiṇai selector** — explore the five landscapes and find your emotional home.
- **Body-as-temple explorer** with a guided breath practice.
- **Behavioural-change engine** — turn each pillar into one small living practice; pledges and reflections are saved privately in your browser (no account, nothing sent anywhere).
- **Accessibility-first** — high-contrast mode, adjustable text size, light/dark themes, reduced-motion support, Tamil-script emphasis, full keyboard navigation, skip links, and ARIA roles throughout.

## Tech

Pure static HTML / CSS / vanilla JavaScript — no build step, no tracking, no dependencies beyond web fonts. Hosted on GitHub Pages.

## Sources

Grounded in the Sangam corpus (Tolkāppiyam, Puṟanāṉūṟu), the Tirukkuṟaḷ, and Siddhar literature (Thirumūlar's *Tirumandiram*, Sivavākkiyar). Full source list is in the site footer.
